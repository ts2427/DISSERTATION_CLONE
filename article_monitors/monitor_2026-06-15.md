## NEW ARTICLES THIS WEEK

### Article N1 — NEW ⭐
**Chen, X., Hilary, G., & Tian, X. (2024).** Mandatory Data Breach Disclosure and Insider Trading. *Journal of Business Finance and Accounting*.
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1111/jbfa.12842
**SSRN:** https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=5012929

**Summary:** Exploiting staggered state-level adoption of data breach notification laws as a quasi-natural experiment, the authors find that mandatory disclosure laws increase insider selling profits, particularly for firms with weaker governance and higher cybersecurity risk. The effect is attenuated in states with stricter enforcement and higher litigation risk, revealing an unintended consequence: laws designed to protect consumers may reduce capital market integrity by incentivizing managerial hedging.

**Relevance Analysis:**
- **H2 (Regulation & returns):** Directly tests how mandatory disclosure laws alter informed-trading dynamics — a novel unintended-consequence angle for the FCC rule analysis.
- **H1 (Firm characteristics):** Governance quality and cybersecurity risk exposure moderate how insiders exploit the regulatory information window.
- **H5 (Information asymmetry):** Insider selling profits proxy for pre-announcement information asymmetry between managers and outside investors.

### Article N2 — NEW ⭐
**Chatterjee, C., Agarwal, N., & Agarwal, S. (2024).** Data Breach Notification Laws and the Cost of Private Debt. *The British Accounting Review*.
**DOI/URL:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0890838924002981
**SSRN:** https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4812852

**Summary:** Using staggered enactment of state data breach notification laws, lenders charge significantly higher loan interest rates after DBN law passage, pricing future litigation and disclosure costs into debt contracts. The cost-of-debt increase is more pronounced for breach-susceptible industries and firms with internal control weaknesses. Firms that proactively appoint a technology officer to the board experience significantly smaller interest rate increases.

**Relevance Analysis:**
- **H2 (Regulation impact):** Demonstrates that mandatory disclosure regulations impose real capital-structure costs that ripple beyond equity markets — strengthens the economic case for studying FCC rule effects.
- **H1 (Firm characteristics):** Board composition (tech officer presence) and internal control quality moderate the regulatory impact on financing costs.
- **H5 (Information asymmetry):** Higher debt costs reflect lender-perceived opacity about cybersecurity vulnerability — a debt-market proxy for H5.

### Article N3 — NEW ⭐
**Hydari, M. Z., Liang, Y., & Telang, R. (2024).** Sound and Fury, Signifying Nothing? Impact of Data Breach Disclosure Laws. *arXiv Working Paper 2406.15215*.
**URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.15215
**SSRN:** https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4864415

**Summary:** Using a diff-in-diff design across 302 stores over a 20-week window following a major U.S. retailer breach, the authors find no evidence that disclosure laws reduce customer revenue — consumers do not punish breached firms by reducing purchases. The paper challenges the standard assumption that the "customer punishment" disciplinary channel motivates regulatory compliance, offering a powerful counterpoint to the pro-disclosure consensus.

**Relevance Analysis:**
- **H2 (Regulation impact):** Essential counterpoint — if the consumer-punishment mechanism is weak, the regulatory discipline effect tested in H2 must operate primarily through financial markets rather than product markets.
- **H1 (Firm characteristics):** Consumer response heterogeneity may be moderated by firm size/reputation, relevant to cross-sectional H1 predictions.
- **FLAG:** ⭐ Useful for identifying the financial-markets channel as the primary enforcement mechanism of disclosure mandates.

### Article N4 — NEW ⭐
**Crosignani, M., Macchiavelli, M., & Silva, A. F. (2023).** Pirates Without Borders: The Propagation of Cyberattacks Through Firms' Supply Chains. *Journal of Financial Economics*, 147(2), 432–448.
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jfineco.2022.10.004
**URL:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0304405X2200246X

**Summary:** Studying the NotPetya cyberattack (historically the most damaging), the paper finds a four-fold amplification of direct profit drops through supply-chain contagion to downstream customers. Losses were largest for customers with fewer alternative suppliers, establishing that attack type and severity determine not only direct target losses but also systemic spillovers. The paper introduces supply-chain contagion as a novel channel by which breach type determines aggregate market impact.

**Relevance Analysis:**
- **H4 (Breach type/severity):** Contributes the supply-chain externality dimension to breach type — devastating infrastructure attacks have multiplier effects beyond the target firm.
- **H5 (Volatility/information asymmetry):** Supply-chain uncertainty creates systemic information asymmetry across affected industries.
- **H1 (Firm characteristics):** Customer firms' supply-chain dependency is a firm characteristic predicting market impact magnitude.

### Article N5 — NEW ⭐
**Akey, P., Lewellen, S., Liskovich, I., & Schiller, C. (2026).** Hacking Corporate Reputations. *Review of Finance*, 30(3), 795–[pages].
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1093/rof/rfae057
**URL:** https://academic.oup.com/rof/article/30/3/795/8529060

**Summary:** Exploiting unexpected corporate data breaches as reputation shocks, the authors find that breaches decrease equity and brand values by 10–20%, increase customer churn, and prompt more negative media coverage. Firms repair reputation by increasing charitable donations, political contributions, employee wages, and IT investment, with responses targeted to the most salient stakeholder groups. The calibration of reputation-repair investment to affected stakeholder type directly links breach type to recovery trajectory.

**Relevance Analysis:**
- **H4 (Breach type):** Breach-as-reputation-shock framework — stakeholder salience (consumers, employees, government) determines whether reputation repair is targeted at product, labor, or regulatory markets.
- **H6 (Executive response):** Charitable and political expenditures represent a form of post-breach governance response alongside executive turnover; the paper complements the CxO-dismissal channel.
- **H1 (Firm characteristics):** Reputation repair scale and type vary with firm size, industry, and stakeholder concentration.
- **FLAG:** ⭐ 2026 publication in a top journal — very recent and directly citable.

### Article N6 — NEW
**Wang, H. E., Wang, Q. E., & Wu, W. (2022).** Short Selling Surrounding Data Breach Announcements. *Finance Research Letters*, 47, Article 102690.
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.frl.2022.102690
**URL:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1544612322000198

**Summary:** The study finds abnormal short-selling activity in the pre-announcement window before data breach disclosures, consistent with informed short sellers anticipating the negative news. Event study results confirm significantly negative CARs around announcements, and pre-announcement short-selling volume strongly predicts the subsequent CAR magnitude. This provides direct evidence that information asymmetry is substantial before breach disclosure, allowing informed traders to profit.

**Relevance Analysis:**
- **H5 (Information asymmetry):** Pre-announcement abnormal short selling is a direct market-microstructure proxy for information asymmetry between insiders and public investors.
- **H1 (Timing):** The existence of pre-disclosure informed trading implies that strategic disclosure timing (H1) could affect the magnitude of short-selling profits.
- **H2 (Regulation):** Mandatory disclosure rules should compress the window in which informed short selling can occur, providing an H5 mechanism for the H2 regulation test.

### Article N7 — NEW ⭐
**Cline, B., Yore, A., & Whited, T. (2022).** Informed Trading in the Options Market Surrounding Data Breaches. *Journal of Empirical Finance*, 68.
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jempfin.2022.04.001
**URL:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S104402832200076X

**Summary:** Using diff-in-diff analysis, the study identifies two groups of pre-announcement traders: hackers who begin informed options trading 8–12 months before breach announcements, and corporate insiders who start approximately 4 months before disclosure. Put-to-call volume ratios predict subsequent negative CARs, providing the strongest microstructure evidence that breaches create persistent, exploitable information asymmetry beginning well before public disclosure.

**Relevance Analysis:**
- **H5 (Information asymmetry):** The distinction between hacker-informed and insider-informed trading provides a granular picture of the information asymmetry lifecycle around breach announcements.
- **H1 (Timing):** The 4-month insider window directly supports the strategic-timing mechanism in H1 — insiders can time disclosure to manage stock price impact.
- **H2 (Regulation):** Mandatory FCC/SEC disclosure rules should narrow or eliminate the window for insider-informed options trading — a testable regulatory mechanism for H2.
- **FLAG:** ⭐ Exceptional options-market evidence for H5 methodology.

### Article N8 — NEW
**Canace, T. G., & Aghazadeh, S. (2021).** Learning from Digital Failures? The Effectiveness of Firms' Divestiture and Management Turnover Responses to Data Breaches. *Strategy Science*, 6(2).
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1287/stsc.2020.0106
**URL:** https://pubsonline.informs.org/doi/10.1287/stsc.2020.0106

**Summary:** Analyzing 8,760 U.S. publicly traded firms at risk of data breaches from 2005–2016, the study finds that unrelated divestitures significantly reduce breach reoccurrence, but CTO turnover alone has no significant effect on preventing future breaches, suggesting that CTO dismissals are largely symbolic responses rather than substantive governance improvements. This finding informs H6 by distinguishing symbolic from substantive executive accountability responses.

**Relevance Analysis:**
- **H6 (Executive turnover):** Crucial nuance — not all executive turnover prevents repeat breaches. Symbolic CTO dismissals differ from structural operational changes (divestitures) in their effectiveness.
- **H3 (Repeat breach):** Firms that respond via divestiture (rather than just CTO replacement) avoid repeat breaches — linking governance response quality to H3 outcomes.
- **H4 (Breach type):** Type of technology failure may determine whether CTO replacement vs. divestiture is the appropriate response.

### Article N9 — NEW
**Durongkadej, I., & Wang, H. E. (2025).** Data Breach Announcement Effect on Bank Loans, Deposits, and Stock Performance. *Journal of Financial Services Research*, 68(3), 423–460.
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s10693-025-00447-z
**URL:** https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10693-025-00447-z

**Summary:** Focusing on breaches at banking institutions, the study finds that breach announcements cause an outflow of insured deposits in the announcement quarter, followed by a decrease in brokered deposits four quarters later. Banks increase lending activity as a compensatory strategy, but stock returns decline on the announcement day and persist negatively for up to three quarters. The paper documents important size and institutional heterogeneity in market reactions specific to financial-sector firms.

**Relevance Analysis:**
- **H1 (Firm characteristics):** Financial-sector institutional characteristics (deposit structure, regulatory oversight) produce distinctively different and more persistent market reactions.
- **H4 (Breach type/sector):** Banking-sector breaches trigger deposit-flight in addition to stock market penalties — a unique mechanism not captured in general-industry studies.
- **H5 (Information asymmetry):** Depositor behavior constitutes a non-equity information asymmetry signal; persistence of stock declines reflects ongoing uncertainty.

### Article N10 ⚑ FLAGGED (2019)
**Corbet, S., & Gurdgiev, C. (2019).** What the Hack: Systematic Risk Contagion from Cyber Events. *International Review of Financial Analysis*, 65, Article 101353.
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.irfa.2019.05.006
**URL:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1057521919300274

**Summary:** Examining equity market volatility consequences of cybercrime for publicly traded corporations, the study finds significant contagion effects: a breach at one firm triggers increased volatility across same-exchange peers and even internationally. The systematic volatility channel is particularly strong for smaller-cap firms, establishing that cybersecurity shocks create systemic information events rather than purely idiosyncratic ones.

**Relevance Analysis:**
- **H5 (Volatility/information asymmetry):** Foundational evidence that breaches produce *systematic* market volatility — important for distinguishing idiosyncratic from systemic H5 effects in the dissertation.
- **H1 (Firm size):** Smaller-cap firms exhibit stronger contagion effects — direct evidence for the H1 size-heterogeneity prediction.
