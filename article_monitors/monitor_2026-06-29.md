### Article N15 — NEW ⭐
**Wang, X., Zhang, X., & Li, C. (2026).** Short-Term Stock Market Reactions to Software Security Defects: An Event Study. *Systems*, 14(1), 14.
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.3390/systems14010014
**URL:** https://www.mdpi.com/2079-8954/14/1/14
**Published:** December 24, 2025 (MDPI Systems Vol. 14, Issue 1, January 2026)

**Summary:** Using a multi-model event-study framework integrating the Constant Mean Return Model (CMRM), ARIMA, and CAPM, the paper examines how three distinct types of software security defects — vulnerability disclosures, active exploitation events, and misconfiguration incidents — affect short-horizon stock market behavior. Vulnerability disclosures produce negative abnormal returns paired with *reduced* trading activity, while exploitation events generate larger price declines accompanied by significant *increases* in trading volume, suggesting different information-asymmetry transmission mechanisms. Misconfiguration incidents show weaker price effects but persistent turnover increases, interpreted as governance-signal events rather than pure security shocks.

**Relevance Analysis:**
- **H4 (Breach type differential effects):** The paper's central contribution — differentiates three security-defect types and demonstrates each produces distinct market reactions. Directly extendable to the broader H4 breach-type taxonomy (financial vs. credential vs. system-deficiency breaches) by analogy with the vulnerability/exploitation/misconfiguration tripartite structure.
- **H5 (Volatility / information asymmetry):** The divergence between vulnerability disclosure (lower volume, negative returns) and exploitation events (higher volume, larger declines) maps precisely to H5's prediction that information asymmetry operates differently depending on whether the event confirms pre-known risk or reveals new information.
- **H1 (Firm characteristics):** Market reactions vary with "industry context and firm role" — cross-sectional heterogeneity consistent with H1 size and sector predictions.
- **H2 (Disclosure regulation):** Mandatory disclosure requirements (SEC Item 1.05, FCC 2024 rule) would compress the gap between vulnerability and exploitation announcements — directly testable using this paper's tripartite typology.
- **FLAG:** ⭐ Methodological contribution — the multi-model event-study (CMRM + ARIMA + CAPM) is a robustness template directly applicable to the dissertation's event-study design for H1 and H5.

### Article N16 — NEW ⭐
**Abrardi, L., Comino, S., & Grassini, S. (2025).** The Economics of Cyber Risk: A Survey of the Literature. *Journal of Industrial and Business Economics*.
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s40812-025-00370-3
**URL:** https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40812-025-00370-3
**Published:** August 19, 2025

**Summary:** A comprehensive survey of the economics of cybersecurity organized around four actors — hackers, companies, consumers/users, and regulators — with particular emphasis on market-failure rationales for mandatory disclosure policy, cyber-insurance market design, and the conditions under which firms under-invest in security. The paper synthesizes the economics literature on how regulatory interventions (including mandatory disclosure mandates) alter investment incentives and market equilibria. It explicitly models the information asymmetry between firms and regulators/investors as a central driver of market outcomes.

**Relevance Analysis:**
- **H2 (FCC/SEC regulation impact):** Provides the microeconomic theory underpinning for why mandatory disclosure regulation should affect stock returns — market-failure and information-externality arguments that motivate the FCC's 2024 rule and the SEC's 2023 rule. Essential for the dissertation's theoretical framing of H2.
- **H5 (Information asymmetry):** Information asymmetry between firms and outside parties (investors, regulators, consumers) is modeled as the central market failure — directly maps to H5's proxies (bid-ask spread, volatility, analyst forecast errors).
- **H1 (Firm size heterogeneity):** The survey discusses firm-size-dependent incentives for cybersecurity investment, supporting the theoretical basis for H1 size-based heterogeneity predictions.
- **FLAG:** ⭐ Theoretical-foundation paper — use for the dissertation's theory section to establish the economic rationale for why regulation (H2), information asymmetry (H5), and firm heterogeneity (H1) are jointly relevant. Complements the empirical systematic reviews (Liu & Babar, 2026; Luo, Chen & Zhang, 2026) with economic theory.

### Article N17 — NEW ⭐⭐
**Luo, Y., Chen, S., & Zhang, P. (2026).** A Review of Research on Data Security Risk: Consequences, Mechanisms, and Response. *Journal of Economic Surveys*.
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1111/joes.70088
**URL:** https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/joes.70088
**Published online:** March 13, 2026

**Summary:** A sweeping systematic review synthesizing over 130 high-quality empirical studies across economics, finance, and management on the determinants and consequences of data security risk. The review documents how data breaches affect firm value and investor behavior, traces the evolution of research methodology from early event studies to NLP/LLM-based disclosure scoring frameworks, and examines governance mechanisms (executive turnover, board changes), insurance markets, and regulatory design as responses to breach risk. The paper is organized around three axes: *consequences* (market, reputational, financial), *mechanisms* (information channels, governance channels, product-market channels), and *responses* (managerial, regulatory, insurance).

**Relevance Analysis:**
- **All hypotheses (H1–H6):** This is the most comprehensive single review paper found to date that spans every dissertation hypothesis. Alongside Liu & Babar (2026, Australian J. Management) and Amani (2025, Accounting Perspectives), it forms a three-way review triumvirate for the dissertation literature-review section.
- **H1 (Timing × firm characteristics):** Reviews the full cross-sectional evidence on how firm size, sector, and governance quality moderate market reactions — directly summarizes the H1 empirical foundation.
- **H2 (Regulation):** Explicitly covers regulatory design as a "response" category, mapping how mandatory disclosure rules alter information dynamics and market equilibria.
- **H3 (Repeat breach):** Reviews the accumulated evidence on how breach history affects firm value — directly synthesizes the H3 literature.
- **H5 (Information asymmetry):** Traces the evolution from bid-ask spread/volume measures to NLP-based asymmetry proxies — methodologically relevant for modernizing the H5 empirical approach.
- **H6 (Executive turnover):** Reviews governance-mechanism studies as one of three major response categories.
- **FLAG:** ⭐⭐ *Highest-priority new citation this week* — most recent comprehensive review (published March 2026, post-dating Liu & Babar). Cite as the state-of-the-art systematic evidence base for the dissertation's entire literature review. Published in *Journal of Economic Surveys* (ABDC A; ABS 3), a top-tier review outlet.
