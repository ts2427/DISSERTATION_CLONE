## SECTION 1 — Market Reactions, Event Studies & Disclosure Timing

---

### Article 1
**Foerderer, J., & Schuetz, S. (2022).** Data Breach Announcements and Stock Market Reactions: A Matter of Timing? *Management Science*, 68(10), 7298–7322.  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.2021.4264  
**URL:** https://pubsonline.informs.org/doi/abs/10.1287/mnsc.2021.4264

**Summary:** Using breach announcements from 2008–2018, the authors show that firms strategically time breach disclosures to coincide with high-news-pressure days (e.g., major concurrent news events), thereby reducing media attention and attenuating negative market reactions. On days with one standard deviation higher predictable news pressure, 4.44% more data breaches are announced. Strategic timing reduces the median market-cap loss from $347 million to $85 million, representing a substantial wealth transfer from investors to managers.

**Relevance Analysis:**
- **H1 (Disclosure timing & firm characteristics):** Directly central — the paper's core contribution is that *when* a firm discloses, not just *what* it discloses, drives market pricing. Strategic timing is more prevalent for severe breaches, internally caused events, and healthcare/credential breaches.
- **H4 (Breach type effects):** Strategic timing varies by breach type (healthcare and credentials vs. financial data).
- **H5 (Information asymmetry):** Strategic timing reduces information quality reaching investors, amplifying asymmetry costs.
- **FLAG:** ⭐ Tier-1 citation — directly addresses H1 and methodology for event study design.

### Article 2
**Kamiya, S., Kang, J.-K., Kim, J., Milidonis, A., & Stulz, R. M. (2021).** Risk management, firm reputation, and the impact of successful cyberattacks on target firms. *Journal of Financial Economics*, 139(3), 719–749.  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jfineco.2020.09.008  
**SSRN:** https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3135514

**Summary:** The paper develops a model in which the nature of the cyberattack (financial information vs. other data) determines the shareholder wealth impact. Empirically, breaches involving loss of personal financial information generate significantly larger and more persistent market penalties than other breach types, far exceeding the direct out-of-pocket costs. Sales growth declines significantly in the two years following financial-information breaches, confirming lasting reputational damage.

**Relevance Analysis:**
- **H1 (Firm characteristics):** Firm reputation, risk management practices, and financial leverage moderate market impact.
- **H4 (Breach type):** Primary contribution — differentiates financial-information breaches from all others with clear pricing effects.
- **H5 (Information asymmetry):** Post-attack decline in sales implies ongoing uncertainty about firm resilience.
- **FLAG:** ⭐ Tier-1 foundational citation for H4 and breach-type heterogeneity analysis.

### Article 3
**Oyima-Antseleve, N. (2025).** Expectations and Market Reactions to Data Breaches. *Journal of Financial Stability* (forthcoming/in press).  
**DOI (SSRN):** https://ssrn.com/abstract=5240536  
**URL:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S154461232502450X

**Summary:** Using breach data from 2005–2019, this paper proposes an expectations-based framework: the market penalty for a breach depends not on its absolute size but on whether breach severity *exceeds* investors' ex-ante expectations. Unexpectedly severe breaches predict subsequent declines in firm sales, confirming that abnormal returns capture real economic harm. Financial-data breaches amplify market reactions to unexpected severity.

**Relevance Analysis:**
- **H1 (Timing & firm characteristics):** Firm-level expectations (formed by size, sector, prior disclosures) shape the cross-sectional variation in market pricing — key for H1.
- **H4 (Breach type):** Financial-data breaches amplify the severity-expectations gap.
- **H3 (Prior breach history):** Prior breaches likely recalibrate investor expectations; this framework supports why repeat breaches may carry smaller or larger penalties depending on expectation updating.
- **FLAG:** ⭐ Reconciles mixed findings in the literature — directly useful for framing H1 and H3 results.

### Article 4
**Akyildirim, E., Conlon, T., Corbet, S., & Hou, Y. (2024).** HACKED: Understanding the stock market response to cyberattacks. *Journal of International Financial Markets, Institutions and Money*, 97, 102082.  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.intfin.2024.102082  
**URL:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1042443124001483

**Summary:** Using a large international sample, the paper finds that stock returns decrease by −0.24% the day after a cyberattack, but this effect reverses within approximately two weeks. The magnitude of the stock market decline is greatest for firms with recurring events and the most severe breaches. Firm-level characteristics — including size, asset volatility, and credit ranking — explain cross-sectional variation in reactions; national institutional and macroeconomic factors do not.

**Relevance Analysis:**
- **H1 (Firm characteristics in pricing):** Firm size, volatility, and credit quality are the dominant cross-sectional moderators — directly supports H1 heterogeneity.
- **H3 (Prior breach history):** Recurring events produce larger declines, providing direct evidence for H3.
- **H4 (Breach severity/type):** Severe breaches generate larger penalties.
- **H5 (Volatility):** Short-term reversal pattern consistent with initial information asymmetry followed by price discovery.

### Article 5
**Tosun, O. K. (2021).** Cyber-attacks and stock market activity. *International Review of Financial Analysis*, 76, 101795.  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.irfa.2021.101795  
**URL:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1057521921001319

**Summary:** Analyzing first-time corporate hacking disclosures, the paper documents that excess returns drop, trading volume rises due to selling pressure, and liquidity deteriorates around announcement. Google search frequency data suggests these market reactions are driven by investor attention. Importantly, cyberattacks affect firm policies (investments, cash holdings) for up to five years post-breach.

**Relevance Analysis:**
- **H5 (Information asymmetry & volatility):** Trading volume increase and liquidity deterioration are direct proxies for elevated information asymmetry and volatility around disclosure.
- **H1 (Timing):** First-time vs. repeat breach distinction is implicit; attention-driven pricing mechanism supports timing effects in H1.
- **H6 (Executive turnover):** Long-run policy effects suggest governance response follows breaches.

### Article 6
**Block, M. (2024).** Market Reactions to Material Cybersecurity Incident Disclosures. *arXiv*, 2512.06144.  
**DOI/URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.06144  
**Institution:** Johns Hopkins University

**Summary:** Examining 54 SEC Form 8-K filings under the new Item 1.05 requirement (2023–2025) covering 36 unique companies, the paper finds that companies experience negative price reactions following mandatory SEC disclosure of material cybersecurity incidents. Smaller companies face more pronounced declines; differences by sector and beta are not statistically significant in this early-period sample.

**Relevance Analysis:**
- **H2 (FCC/SEC regulation impact on returns):** Directly tests the new 2023 SEC mandatory disclosure rule — the closest available parallel to FCC rules for H2.
- **H1 (Firm size in market pricing):** Smaller firms suffer larger price drops post-mandatory disclosure, an H1 finding for the regulated regime.
- **FLAG:** ⭐ Most current evidence on mandatory SEC disclosure market effects — essential for H2 comparison with FCC rules.

### Article 7
**Mukit, M. A. K. et al. (2025).** The Dynamics of Stock Market Responses Following the Cyber-Attacks News: Evidence from Event Study. *Information Systems Frontiers*.  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s10796-025-10639-6  
**URL:** https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10796-025-10639-6

**Summary:** An event study of 30+ cyberattack events from 2018–2024 using abnormal return (AR) and cumulative abnormal return (CAR) estimation finds a consistent short-term negative effect on stock prices, particularly acute in the financial sector. Technology companies show faster recovery. Results confirm sector-specific heterogeneity in breach market impact.

**Relevance Analysis:**
- **H4 (Breach type/sector effects):** Financial sector suffers most; technology recovers fastest — directly supports sector-heterogeneity analysis.
- **H1 (Firm characteristics):** Sector identity is a key firm characteristic affecting pricing.
- **H5 (Volatility):** Event study methodology directly measures abnormal return volatility around cyber events.

## SECTION 2 — Regulatory Environment: FCC, SEC & Disclosure Mandates

---

### Article 8
**Cao, H., Phan, H. V., & Silveri, S. (2024).** Data breach disclosures and stock price crash risk: Evidence from data breach notification laws. *International Review of Financial Analysis*, 93, 103164.  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.irfa.2024.103164  
**SSRN:** https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4393481

**Summary:** Using U.S. state-level adoption of data breach notification (DBN) laws as a quasi-natural experiment, the authors find that mandatory disclosure laws *increase* future stock price crash risk. The effect is more pronounced for firms with weaker corporate governance, higher financial constraints, and higher information asymmetry. The mechanism is investor concern about firm vulnerability — mandatory disclosure amplifies tail risk perceptions.

**Relevance Analysis:**
- **H2 (Regulation impact on returns):** Central paper — tests how mandatory disclosure regulation affects stock market outcomes. Paradoxically, disclosure mandates worsen crash risk rather than improving market efficiency, a nuance critical for H2.
- **H5 (Information asymmetry):** The asymmetry-moderating result confirms that high-asymmetry firms are disproportionately affected by disclosure mandates.
- **H1 (Firm characteristics):** Governance quality and financial constraints interact with regulatory effects — directly supports H1 cross-sectional prediction.

### Article 9
**Boasiako, K. A., & Keefe, M. O. (2021).** Data breaches and corporate liquidity management. *European Financial Management*, 27(3), 528–551.  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1111/eufm.12289  
**URL:** https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/eufm.12289

**Summary:** Using state-level mandatory disclosure law passage as an exogenous shock, the paper shows that firms respond by increasing cash holdings and reducing external financing and investment. Breach-experiencing firms further amplify these precautionary financial adjustments. The results document significant financial policy disruption from both regulation and realized breaches.

**Relevance Analysis:**
- **H2 (Regulation impact):** Disclosure mandates demonstrably affect firm financial policies — relevant counterfactual for stock return studies.
- **H1 (Firm characteristics):** Cash-constrained firms (smaller firms) face greater disruption, supporting size-based heterogeneity.
- **H5 (Information asymmetry):** Increased cash holdings reflect elevated uncertainty and reduced external financing access post-breach.

### Article 10
**Zhu, X. (2025).** Data Breach Notification Laws and Corporate Tax Planning. *Journal of Corporate Accounting & Finance*.  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1002/jcaf.22767  
**URL:** https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/jcaf.22767

**Summary:** When a firm's headquarters is in a state with data breach notification laws, the long-term effective tax rate decreases by approximately 3.8% (~$8.13M annually). Mandatory disclosure laws are associated with less aggressive tax avoidance, suggesting firms shift compliance resources toward cybersecurity governance rather than tax minimization strategies.

**Relevance Analysis:**
- **H2 (Regulation impact):** Demonstrates that disclosure mandates reshape firm financial decision-making broadly, beyond direct market pricing — useful for robustness discussion.
- **H1 (Firm characteristics):** Firm size and tax position moderate the regulatory compliance response.

### Article 11
**Amani, F. (2025).** Cybersecurity Risks and Incidents Disclosure: A Literature Review. *Accounting Perspectives*, 24(1).  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1111/1911-3838.12411

**FCC Rule Summary (Policy Reference):**

The FCC finalized expanded data breach notification rules in December 2023 (effective March 13, 2024; Federal Register 2024-01667). Key features:
- Expanded scope from CPNI to all personally identifiable information (PII)
- 7-business-day notification deadline to FCC, Secret Service, and FBI for breaches affecting ≥500 customers
- 30-day customer notification deadline
- Upheld by the Sixth Circuit Court of Appeals (August 13, 2025)

## SECTION 3 — Information Asymmetry, Volatility & Market Microstructure

---

### Article 12
**Florackis, C., Louca, C., Michaely, R., & Weber, M. (2023).** Cybersecurity Risk. *Review of Financial Studies*, 36(1), 351–407.  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1093/rfs/hhac024  
**URL:** https://academic.oup.com/rfs/article-abstract/36/1/351/6585907

**Summary:** Constructing a novel firm-level cybersecurity risk measure from textual analysis of 10-K "Risk Factors" disclosures, the paper shows that cybersecurity risk is priced in the cross-section of stock returns. Firms with higher cybersecurity risk disclosure earn higher subsequent returns consistent with a risk premium. The measure predicts future cyberattacks, is more prevalent in IT-intensive industries, and has trended upward over time.

**Relevance Analysis:**
- **H5 (Information asymmetry):** Cybersecurity risk as disclosed in filings drives a priced risk premium — firms with more opaque cyber postures command higher required returns, implying elevated information asymmetry costs.
- **H1 (Firm characteristics):** Industry, IT-intensity, and disclosure quality are predictors of cyber risk exposure and pricing.
- **H2 (Regulation):** Baseline measure of cyber risk before and after disclosure mandates — useful for pre/post regulatory comparison in H2.

### Article 13
**Kwon, J., & Lee, J. (2021).** The Impact of Executives' IT Expertise on Reported Data Security Breaches. *Information Systems Research*, 32(2), 318–334.  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1287/isre.2020.0986  
**URL:** https://dl.acm.org/doi/abs/10.1287/isre.2020.0986

**Summary:** Analyzing 2005–2017 breach data, the paper shows that data security breach risk is a shared top-management-team responsibility (CEO, CFO, CIO), with CEOs possessing IT expertise associated with significantly fewer breaches. The paper documents that TMT composition systematically predicts breach incidence, making it a predictor of the firm-level characteristics that drive both H5 and H6.

**Relevance Analysis:**
- **H6 (Executive turnover):** Establishes that IT expertise in the executive suite prevents breaches — by implication, lack of expertise predicts post-breach turnover pressure.
- **H5 (Information asymmetry):** TMT composition moderates breach risk, which in turn drives information environment quality.
- **H1 (Firm characteristics):** Governance quality (executive expertise) is a firm characteristic affecting breach vulnerability and market impact.

## SECTION 4 — Repeat Breaches & Prior Breach History

---

### Article 14
**Peng, J., Zhang, H., Mao, J., & Xu, S. (2023).** How do repeated data breaches affect firm policies? *Applied Economics Letters*, 32(1).  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1080/13504851.2023.2257027  
**URL:** https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/13504851.2023.2257027

**Summary:** A second data breach experienced by a firm decreases firm value more than the first, and this penalty is most pronounced when financial information is involved. The paper also documents changes in firm financial and operational policies following repeat breaches. CEO turnover after the first breach can partially insulate firm value from the impact of subsequent breaches.

**Relevance Analysis:**
- **H3 (Prior breach history penalty):** Direct and primary evidence — repeat breaches incur larger market penalties than first breaches.
- **H4 (Breach type):** Financial-information breaches amplify the repeat-breach penalty.
- **H6 (Executive turnover):** CEO replacement after first breach is shown to moderate the repeat-breach market reaction — bridges H3 and H6.
- **FLAG:** ⭐ Key citation for H3 — directly tests the repeat-breach hypothesis.

### Article 15 *(Related — Repeat Breach, Executive Compensation)*
**Peng, J., Zhang, H., Mao, J., & Xu, S. (2024).** Repeated data breaches and executive compensation. *Applied Economics Letters*, 32(8).  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1080/13504851.2024.2302552  
**URL:** https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/13504851.2024.2302552

**Summary:** A follow-on study showing that executive compensation structures respond to repeat data breaches — pay-performance sensitivity decreases and equity-based compensation is altered following a second breach. The results suggest boards discipline management through compensation after repeat incidents, even if outright turnover is less common.

**Relevance Analysis:**
- **H3 (Repeat breach penalty):** Complements Article 14 by showing the governance channel (compensation) through which repeat-breach penalties operate.
- **H6 (Executive turnover):** Compensation adjustment is a softer governance response that may precede or substitute for outright turnover.

## SECTION 5 — Executive Turnover & Corporate Governance

---

### Article 16
**Zhou, F., & Huang, J. (2024).** Cybersecurity data breaches and internal control. *International Review of Financial Analysis*, 93, 103174.  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.irfa.2024.103174  
**URL:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1057521924001066

**Summary:** Data breaches are negatively associated with subsequent internal control material weaknesses — i.e., breached firms improve their internal controls. CEO dismissal following a breach is identified as an effective mechanism for driving this internal control improvement. Firms with dismissed CEOs are less likely to file "Big R" restatements and face litigation post-breach.

**Relevance Analysis:**
- **H6 (Executive turnover):** Directly demonstrates that CEO dismissal is a governance mechanism following data breaches, and that turnover produces measurable improvement in internal controls.
- **H2 (Regulation impact):** Regulatory compliance and internal control quality are downstream effects of the breach-and-respond cycle that disclosure mandates accelerate.
- **FLAG:** ⭐ Core citation for H6 — establishes CEO turnover as a post-breach governance response with measurable firm-level consequences.

## SECTION 6 — Flagged Highly Relevant Work from 2015–2020

### Article 18 ⚑ FLAGGED (2019)
**Banker, R. D., & Feng, C. (2019).** The Impact of Information Security Breach Incidents on CIO Turnover. *Journal of Information Systems*, 33(3), 309–329.  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.2308/isys-52532  
**URL:** https://publications.aaahq.org/jis/article-abstract/33/3/309/1479

**Summary:** Finds that data security breaches caused by system deficiency (as opposed to human error or malicious outsiders) increase CIO turnover likelihood by 72%. Breaches resulting from complex IT environments increase CIO replacement probability, while simpler operational breaches are attributed differently. Establishes the causal pathway from breach type to executive accountability.

**Relevance Analysis:**
- **H6 (Executive turnover):** Foundational empirical evidence on CIO turnover post-breach — establishes that breach type moderates which executive faces accountability.
- **H4 (Breach type):** System-deficiency breaches vs. human-error breaches produce different executive accountability outcomes — bridges H4 and H6.

### Article 19 ⚑ FLAGGED (2017)
**Goel, S., & Shawky, H. A. (2009 seminal; updated evidence in 2017 literature).** *Referenced as foundational market microstructure study:*  
**Arcuri, M. C., Brogi, M., & Gandolfi, G. (2017).** The effect of data breach announcements beyond the stock price: Empirical evidence on market activity. *International Review of Financial Analysis*, 49, 146–154.  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.irfa.2017.01.001  
**URL:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1057521917300029

**Summary:** Event study of 74 data breaches (2005–2014) demonstrates that breach announcements significantly increase bid-ask spreads and trading volume in the short run. The paper is the first to analyze data breach announcements as a source of asymmetric information between market makers and uninformed liquidity traders, providing a market microstructure perspective on breach information.

**Relevance Analysis:**
- **H5 (Information asymmetry costs):** Foundational evidence that breaches widen bid-ask spreads and increase trading volume — the two primary market-microstructure proxies for information asymmetry.
- **FLAG:** ⭐ This is the canonical paper for H5 methodology — must-cite even though pre-2021.

**Lending, C., Minnick, K., & Schorno, P. J. (2018).** Corporate governance, social responsibility, and data breaches. *Financial Review*, 53(2), 413–455.

**Summary:** Firms that experience data breaches have larger boards with less financial expertise in the pre-breach period. Post-breach, firms make significant governance improvements: smaller boards, more financial expertise, and formalized audit-committee cybersecurity oversight. CEO replacement is the strongest single predictor of improved subsequent governance.
