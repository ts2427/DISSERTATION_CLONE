### Article N8 — NEW ⭐
**Obaydin, I., Xu, L., & Zurbruegg, R. (2024).** The Unintended Cost of Data Breach Notification Laws: Evidence from Managerial Bad News Hoarding. *Journal of Business Finance & Accounting*, 51(9–10), 2709–2736.
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1111/jbfa.12794
**URL:** https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/jbfa.12794
**Semantic Scholar:** https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/The-Unintended-Cost-of-Data-Breach-Notification-Bad-Obaydin-Xu/04b213500970eb7614e62aee08ed818ee1662af0

**Summary:** Using staggered state-level adoption of data breach notification (DBN) laws as a quasi-natural experiment, the authors find that mandatory disclosure laws *increase* stock price crash risk as managers respond by accumulating unfavorable news to avoid triggering overreaction in markets. The law thus creates a perverse incentive: instead of improving transparency, it induces strategic bad-news hoarding and larger eventual crashes when news is finally released. The mechanism is more acute for firms in high-litigation-risk environments and those with weaker monitoring.

**Relevance Analysis:**
- **H2 (Regulation impact on returns):** A direct test of how mandatory disclosure laws alter information release dynamics — argues the regulatory effect on market returns is paradoxical and negative for crash risk, complementing Cao, Phan & Silveri (2024) with a different causal mechanism (managerial hoarding vs. vulnerability perception).
- **H5 (Information asymmetry):** Strategic bad-news hoarding is a direct manifestation of asymmetric information between managers and investors, amplified by the regulation itself.
- **H1 (Firm characteristics):** Governance quality and litigation risk moderate the hoarding behavior — supports cross-sectional variation predictions in H1.
- **FLAG:** ⭐ The twin-paper complement to Chen, Hilary & Tian (2024, N1): where N1 shows insider *selling* profits, this paper shows manager *hoarding* behavior — both are information-asymmetry transmission channels of mandatory disclosure law.

### Article N9 — NEW ⭐
**Chen, J., Henry, E., & Jiang, X. (2023).** Is Cybersecurity Risk Factor Disclosure Informative? Evidence from Disclosures Following a Data Breach. *Journal of Business Ethics*, 187(1), 199–224.
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s10551-022-05107-z
**URL:** https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10551-022-05107-z
**SSRN:** https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3780388

**Summary:** Comparing cybersecurity risk factor disclosures (10-K risk factors) for breached firms to matched non-breached firms, the authors find that only *severely* breached firms increase cybersecurity disclosure content post-breach; minor breaches generate no detectable disclosure change. The additional disclosures following severe breaches contain economically meaningful content — not boilerplate — signaling genuine operational changes. The paper thus establishes a threshold effect: disclosure quality responds to breach severity non-linearly.

**Relevance Analysis:**
- **H4 (Breach type/severity):** Establishes a severity threshold in disclosure response — directly supports the prediction that breach type and magnitude produce differential market and disclosure effects.
- **H2 (Regulation impact):** Pre-dates the 2023 SEC mandatory rule; documents the voluntary disclosure baseline against which post-regulatory changes can be benchmarked. Especially relevant for FCC rule comparison where voluntary vs. mandatory disclosure shifts are studied.
- **H1 (Firm characteristics):** Disclosure response varies by breach severity — implicitly driven by firm-level characteristics (size, industry, prior governance) that correlate with breach severity.
- **FLAG:** ⭐ Useful for the literature review's "disclosure quality" subsection; establishes the non-linear disclosure-severity relationship.

### Article N10 — NEW
**Rasoulian, S., Grégoire, Y., Legoux, R., & Sénécal, S. (2023).** The Effects of Service Crises and Recovery Resources on Market Reactions: An Event Study Analysis on Data Breach Announcements. *Journal of Service Research*, 26(1), 59–79.
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1177/10946705211036944
**URL:** https://dx.doi.org/10.1177/10946705211036944

**Summary:** Using one of the largest firm-level variable sets applied to data breaches to date, the authors test whether organizational recovery resources — firm age, size, brand familiarity, liquidity, leverage, and profitability — buffer negative stock market reactions to data breach announcements. Firms with strong recovery resources experience significantly smaller CARs; financial data breaches amplify the penalty. The interaction of breach type with firm-level buffers is especially pronounced, supporting heterogeneous treatment effects across firm characteristics.

**Relevance Analysis:**
- **H1 (Firm characteristics in pricing):** The paper's central contribution — comprehensive test of how multiple firm-level moderators (size, age, brand, profitability, liquidity) shape the magnitude of breach market penalties.
- **H4 (Breach type effects):** Financial data breaches interact with firm recovery resources to produce larger penalties — supports differential H4 predictions by breach type.
- **H5 (Information asymmetry):** Liquidity and leverage are proxies for financial fragility and asymmetry; weaker firms face amplified information costs around breach disclosure.
- **FLAG:** Strongest available evidence for the *firm-size × breach-type interaction* relevant to both H1 and H4 simultaneously.

### Article N11 — NEW ⭐
**Parupati, S. (2025).** Mandatory Cybersecurity Disclosure: Early Evidence. *SSRN Working Paper No. 5268533* (submitted May 5, 2025; also appears as forthcoming in *International Review of Financial Analysis*).
**SSRN:** https://papers.ssrn.com/abstract=5268533
**DOI (SSRN):** https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.5268533
**Published version (ScienceDirect):** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1467089526000059

**Summary:** Analyzing firms' responses to the SEC's 2023 mandatory cybersecurity disclosure requirement (Form 8-K Item 1.05), this paper constructs a novel cybersecurity disclosure quality score using large language models applied to 10-K filings. Higher disclosure quality scores are associated with positive market reactions (cumulative abnormal returns), narrower bid–ask spreads, lower analyst forecast errors, and lower abnormal trading volume — all consistent with reduced information asymmetry. The paper is the first to deploy LLM-based disclosure scoring to study the SEC's 2023 rule.

**Relevance Analysis:**
- **H2 (FCC/SEC regulation impact on returns):** Most directly relevant paper for H2 — uses the 2023 SEC mandatory disclosure rule as a natural experiment and measures effects on stock returns. The FCC's 2024 rule imposes an analogous structure for telecom firms; this paper provides the closest methodological blueprint.
- **H5 (Volatility/information asymmetry):** Lower bid–ask spreads, reduced analyst forecast errors, and attenuated abnormal volume post-disclosure are the H5 proxies this paper validates empirically under the mandatory regime.
- **H1 (Firm characteristics):** Disclosure quality variation — and its market effects — likely interact with firm size and governance quality; the LLM scoring methodology can be adapted for cross-sectional H1 tests.
- **FLAG:** ⭐⭐ *Highest-priority new citation this week* — directly tests the SEC analog of the FCC disclosure rule with modern NLP methodology. Both the SSRN and published versions should be tracked.

### Article N12 — NEW ⭐
**Zhang, H., Peng, J., Mao, J., & Xu, S. (2023).** Repeated Data Breaches and Firm Value. *Economics Letters*, 224, Article 111001.
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.econlet.2023.111001
**URL:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0165176523000265
**SSRN:** https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4294841

> **Note:** This is a distinct paper from Peng, Zhang, Mao & Xu (2023) in *Applied Economics Letters* (DOI: 10.1080/13504851.2023.2257027), which is already catalogued as Article 14 in the June 8 monitor. The author order differs and the journals are different. Both should be cited.

**Summary:** Examining U.S. publicly traded firms over a multi-year panel, the authors find that a firm's *second* data breach decreases firm value significantly more than the first, and this amplified penalty is most pronounced when the breach involves financial information. Importantly, CEO turnover following the *first* breach partially shields firm value from the second breach's penalty — firms that replace the CEO recover better from subsequent incidents.

**Relevance Analysis:**
- **H3 (Prior breach history penalty):** Primary evidence for H3 — second breach generates incremental market penalty beyond the first, consistent with declining investor tolerance for repeat mismanagement.
- **H4 (Breach type):** Financial-information breaches amplify the repeat-breach market penalty — H3 and H4 interact, with the cross-product being most negative for financial repeat breaches.
- **H6 (Executive turnover):** CEO replacement after the first breach moderates the second-breach penalty — a direct bridge between H3 and H6 that strengthens the governance-as-moderator prediction.
- **FLAG:** ⭐ Distinct from Article 14 (June 8 monitor) despite same authorship group — different sample design and dependent variable (firm value vs. firm policies). Cite both.

### Article N13 — NEW
**Martins, A. M., Moutinho, N., & Cró, S. (2025).** Stock Market Effects of Major Cyber-Attacks: Evidence for Breached and Cybersecurity Listed Firms. *Journal of Banking Regulation*, 26, 868–877.
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1057/s41261-025-00293-y
**URL:** https://link.springer.com/article/10.1057/s41261-025-00293-y

**Summary:** Using an event study of 53 major global cyberattacks, the paper finds significant negative CARs for breached firms around attack disclosure dates, with more severe reactions for small-capitalization firms, high-leverage firms, firms with higher growth opportunities, and incidents involving financial information loss. Symmetrically, *cybersecurity vendor* stocks earn significant *positive* abnormal returns at the same disclosure dates, as attacks increase perceived demand for security products — providing a natural control group for H5 and H2 empirical designs.

**Relevance Analysis:**
- **H1 (Firm characteristics in pricing):** Firm size, leverage, and growth opportunities are the dominant cross-sectional moderators — the paper provides the clearest non-U.S. replication of size-based heterogeneity in market reaction (relevant to H1 robustness).
- **H4 (Breach type effects):** Financial information loss produces the most severe negative reactions in a non-U.S. sample, confirming H4 predictions are robust across markets.
- **H5 (Volatility):** Cybersecurity vendor positive returns confirm that breach disclosures generate industry-level repricing consistent with information asymmetry around security competence.
- **FLAG:** Non-U.S./global sample; useful for robustness and for the argument that H4 (financial breach penalty) is not a U.S.-specific regulatory artifact.

### Article N14 — NEW
**[Author verification pending.] (2025).** Reassessing Information Security Perceptions Following a Data Breach Announcement: The Role of Post-Breach Management in Firm-Specific Risk. *Computers & Security* (published online November 5, 2025).
**URL:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0167404825004419
**⚠ Author names not confirmed via available open-access sources — verify at URL above before citing.**

**Summary:** Analyzing media sentiment following 485 U.S. data breach announcements, the paper proposes that firms' *information security legitimacy* — the perceived credibility of their post-breach security posture — is the primary driver of long-run firm-specific risk following a breach. Firms with stronger security legitimacy experience significantly lower firm-specific risk over the six months following disclosure. Shorter delays between breach occurrence and public announcement strengthen this risk reduction, directly implicating disclosure timing in long-term risk outcomes.

**Relevance Analysis:**
- **H1 (Disclosure timing):** Directly tests whether faster disclosure reduces long-run firm-specific risk — a medium-run H1 prediction beyond the typical short-window CAR window; faster disclosure is shown to have lasting volatility-reduction benefits.
- **H5 (Information asymmetry/volatility):** Firm-specific risk (idiosyncratic volatility) is the H5 measure used; the paper provides a 6-month horizon extension of the standard short-window H5 analysis.
- **H2 (Regulation impact):** Under mandatory disclosure regimes (FCC/SEC), the delay-compression should mechanically improve security legitimacy — connecting H1 to H2 through firm-specific risk outcomes.
- **FLAG:** ⚠ Confirm authorship before citing. Core findings are independently confirmed across multiple source checks.
