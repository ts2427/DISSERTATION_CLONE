## Part One: The Problem — Why This Matters

Every major federal regulator in the United States operates on the same assumption: when a company suffers a data breach, faster disclosure is categorically better. The Federal Communications Commission requires disclosure within seven days of breach discovery. The Securities and Exchange Commission, in its 2023 rule, requires disclosure within four days of determining the breach is material. The Department of Health and Human Services, under HIPAA, allows sixty days.

Three different agencies. Three different timelines. One shared belief — that speed is beneficial, and that more speed is more beneficial.

That belief has never been rigorously tested for causal effects. Not once. Every timing mandate currently in force was designed and implemented without causal evidence that faster disclosure actually improves market outcomes, reduces investor harm, or protects affected stakeholders better than a longer timeline would. Regulators assumed it. They did not prove it.

This dissertation provides that evidence. Across 1,054 data breach disclosures at publicly traded firms spanning two decades — from 2006 to 2025 — the empirical evidence shows that the assumption is wrong. Disclosure timing does not drive market penalties. The regulatory apparatus built around timing does. And that apparatus generates approximately 1.15 billion dollars in unintended economic costs across three independent mechanisms.

Why does this matter beyond academia? Because the SEC's 2023 four-day disclosure rule now applies to every publicly traded firm in the United States. That rule was adopted without this causal evidence. This dissertation provides the first causal test of timing assumptions using the FCC 7-Day Rule as a natural experiment — and the evidence predicts that the SEC rule will produce larger unintended costs than the FCC regime, not smaller ones. Policymakers made consequential decisions without this evidence. Now they have it.

## Part Two: Why This Gap Exists — The Identification Problem

The breach disclosure market reaction literature dates back to 2004. There are hundreds of published studies documenting that breach announcements produce negative stock price reactions. State notification law studies show that jurisdictions with mandatory disclosure laws have different market outcomes than jurisdictions without them. But those studies cannot isolate timing as the variable of interest, because the laws they study vary on multiple dimensions simultaneously.

State notification laws differ in timing requirements, disclosure content, notification scope, and investigation windows. When everything changes at once, you cannot attribute outcomes to timing specifically. You are measuring the bundle, not the timing. This is the foundational methodological gap.

This dissertation closes that gap using the FCC 7-Day Rule, implemented September 28, 2007 (Obaydin et al., 2024; Cao et al., 2024). That rule is a clean regulatory shock — one variable, one date, one population. Telecommunications and cable firms became subject to a mandatory seven-day disclosure deadline. Other firms did not. The variation is regulatory, not self-selected. That is what makes causal identification possible.

Three sources of methodological novelty distinguish this dissertation from prior work (Liu & Babar, 2024; Cavusoglu et al., 2004; Acquisti et al., 2006):

First, the natural experiment itself. The FCC 7-Day Rule has never been used as an identification strategy in the breach disclosure literature. Prior studies use state notification laws, which combine multiple regulatory changes. The FCC rule is cleaner and more administratively defined.

Second, CVSS severity scores. The Common Vulnerability Scoring System, maintained by the National Vulnerability Database, provides a standardized measure of technical breach complexity on a scale from zero to ten. No prior study in the breach market reactions literature has used CVSS scores to distinguish information-based pricing mechanisms from complexity-based pricing. These scores are critical for observing expectation-based pricing — the core mechanism of Essays 1 and 2.

Third, the three-mechanism framework. Prior studies examine market valuation, or market uncertainty, or governance response — separately, in different papers, on different samples. This dissertation examines all three simultaneously on the same 1,054-breach sample, using the same natural experiment, connecting them to a single theoretical argument. That integration of mechanisms is new in the cybersecurity disclosure literature.

## Part Three: The Theoretical Spine — Information Asymmetry and Mandatory Disclosure

Understanding what mandatory timing regulation does to disclosure requires understanding what voluntary timing accomplishes first. The theoretical spine runs through four foundational papers, each building on the last. Together they generate the precise predictions this dissertation tests and confirms.

### Akerlof (1970) — The Market for Lemons

George Akerlof published his famous paper on the market for lemons in 1970. The lemons paper is about used cars, but the insight applies to any market where sellers possess more information than buyers about quality (Akerlof, 1970).

Here is the problem. Imagine a used car market with two types of cars: good cars worth ten thousand dollars and bad cars — lemons — worth two thousand dollars. Sellers know which type they have. Buyers cannot tell. What happens?

Buyers, knowing they might get a lemon, will not pay ten thousand dollars for any car. They will offer something in between — say, six thousand. But sellers of good cars will not sell at six thousand — their car is worth ten thousand. So good cars leave the market. Now only lemons are for sale. Buyers figure this out and lower their offer to two thousand. The market for good cars collapses entirely, even though good cars exist and buyers want them.

Information asymmetry does not just create mispricing. It destroys markets. That is Akerlof's fundamental contribution.

Now apply that to data breaches. A firm that has suffered a minor breach — limited scope, quick remediation, low risk to customers — wants investors to know the situation is contained. A firm with a severe breach wants to delay that knowledge as long as possible. If investors cannot distinguish the two, they assume the worst about every breach disclosure. The good firms — the ones with minor breaches — cannot credibly signal their quality. Markets react as if every breach is severe (Martins et al., 2025; Edwards et al., 2016). That is Akerlof's adverse selection problem applied to cybersecurity.

### Spence (1973) — Costly Signaling

Michael Spence solved Akerlof's problem in 1973 with costly signaling theory (Spence, 1973). Spence showed that if high-quality types can send a signal that is genuinely costly to mimic, then the signal separates quality types even without direct observation of quality. His example was education — a college degree that takes real effort to earn. The key requirement is that the signal must be voluntary, and it must be more costly for low-quality types to send than high-quality types.

Applied to breach disclosure: voluntary early disclosure is a signal. It costs something to disclose early. You risk admitting a problem before investigation is complete, before you know the full scope, before remediation is underway. A firm confident the breach is contained can afford that cost — early disclosure confirms their confidence. A firm with a severe breach cannot afford to disclose early without exposing the full extent of the damage. So early voluntary disclosure separates the firms with contained breaches from the firms with severe ones.

That is the Spence mechanism. Voluntary timing carries information precisely because it is voluntary. The cost differential between firm types is what makes the signal credible.

And it breaks down entirely under mandatory disclosure. When the FCC requires every firm to disclose within seven days, timing is no longer voluntary (Skinner, 1994; Kothari et al., 2009). It is a legal obligation. Every firm discloses early, regardless of whether their breach is contained or severe. The cost differential disappears. The signal is destroyed. Early disclosure no longer means anything, because every firm discloses early whether they want to or not. This is the core prediction of H1.

### Myers and Majluf (1984) — Managerial Private Information

Stewart Myers and Nicholas Majluf made this argument formal in 1984 in the context of corporate financing decisions (Myers & Majluf, 1984). Their insight was that the timing of equity issuance signals private managerial information about firm value.

Here is the logic. Managers know things about firm value that outside investors do not — upcoming contracts, cost structures, growth prospects. When a firm issues new equity, rational investors interpret that decision as a signal. Why would a manager sell shares now? Because the manager thinks the shares are overvalued. Investors know this, so equity issuance is interpreted as a negative signal, and the stock price falls.

The mechanism is identical to what happens with breach disclosure timing. The manager knows breach severity before the market does. Voluntary early disclosure signals that the manager believes the situation is contained — otherwise, why rush to disclose? Voluntary delayed disclosure signals the opposite — or at minimum, that the investigation is still ongoing.

Mandatory timing eliminates the voluntary choice. When every firm is legally required to disclose within seven days, disclosure timing no longer conveys managerial private information. The signal is gone. Markets can no longer use timing to infer severity.

This dissertation extends the Myers-Majluf framework to cybersecurity regulation. Same mechanism, new domain. And the prediction is clear: once mandatory regulation eliminates voluntary timing, timing should carry no significant effect on market valuations. That is exactly what the H1 null result confirms. Timing coefficient: plus 0.57 percent, p-value 0.539. Not significant.

### Diamond and Verrecchia (1991) — The Forced Disclosure Paradox

Douglas Diamond and Robert Verrecchia published their disclosure model in 1991 (Diamond & Verrecchia, 1991). Their central insight was that the relationship between disclosure and information asymmetry is not monotonic — more disclosure does not always mean less asymmetry.

Here is the paradox. Voluntary non-disclosure is rational when disclosure costs are high. Mandatory disclosure regimes can improve market outcomes by eliminating strategic withholding. So far, so good — that is the standard policy justification for disclosure mandates (Gordon et al., 2024; Fabrizio & Kim, 2019).

But the model assumes firms disclose complete information when they do disclose. It does not account for what happens when mandatory timing forces disclosure before investigation is complete. When organizations face binding deadlines shorter than their investigation horizon, the result is not faster complete disclosure. It is on-time incomplete disclosure (Claeys & Cauberghe, 2012; Iqbal et al., 2024). The information gap persists under a different form. And incomplete disclosure under mandatory timing can actually increase information asymmetry relative to complete voluntary disclosure — because investors now face both the bad news of the breach and the uncertainty of not knowing the full scope.

This is the forced disclosure paradox. The regulation designed to reduce information asymmetry increases it (Tsang et al., 2023). Diamond and Verrecchia predicted it theoretically. Essay 2 of this dissertation confirms it empirically for the first time in a cybersecurity regulatory context. H5 coefficient: plus 1.83 percent volatility increase, p-value 0.047.

### Tushman and Nadler (1978) — Organizational Information Processing Capacity

Michael Tushman and David Nadler published their organizational information processing theory in 1978 (Tushman & Nadler, 1978). Their argument was that organizations process, interpret, and communicate information subject to capacity limits, and these limits bind most tightly under time pressure in complex environments.

Large firms have parallel processing capacity — they can run investigation and disclosure simultaneously, maintaining quality in both. Small firms cannot. When a mandatory deadline forces disclosure before investigation concludes, small firms face a choice: comply with the timeline and release incomplete information, or miss the deadline (Hunt et al., 2025; Sanner et al., 2025). They comply. And incomplete disclosure leaves investors uncertain.

This capacity constraint is what produces the firm-size gradient in Essay 2. The smallest firms — first quartile by total assets — experience volatility increases of plus 7.31 percent (p = 0.0032). The second quartile experiences plus 3.64 percent (p = 0.0138). The third quartile shows negative 0.54 percent — not significant (p = 0.77). The largest firms — fourth quartile — experience negative 3.39 percent (p = 0.0149).

That monotonic pattern — large increases for small firms, reversal for large firms — is the signature of an organizational capacity constraint. One-size-fits-all deadlines do not fit all firms. They impose disproportionate costs on the firms with the least capacity to absorb them. This is a testable prediction of Tushman and Nadler. Essay 2 confirms it.

### Mitchell, Agle, and Wood (1997) — Stakeholder Salience and Governance

Ronald Mitchell, Bradley Agle, and Donna Wood published their stakeholder salience theory in 1997 (Mitchell et al., 1997). Their framework identifies three attributes that determine how much organizational attention a stakeholder receives: power, legitimacy, and urgency. Stakeholders with all three attributes — definitive stakeholders — command immediate managerial attention.

Before a data breach, regulators have power and legitimacy but low urgency (Freeman, 1984; Donaldson & Preston, 1995). Their enforcement timelines are long. They are latent stakeholders in the background.

After a mandatory disclosure requirement is triggered, regulators gain urgency. The seven-day FCC deadline transforms regulatory notification from a discretionary act to a legal obligation with immediate consequences (Coombs, 2007). Regulators become definitive stakeholders overnight. And definitive stakeholders get responded to — which is why mandatory disclosure accelerates executive governance changes.

This is the mechanism behind Essay 3. Mandatory timing does not improve governance through better information. It improves governance through stakeholder pressure (Michel et al., 2020). And because urgency is time-bounded — it decays as the crisis recedes — the governance response is transient rather than sustained. H6 coefficient: plus 5.3 percentage points at 30 days (p = 0.008), declining to plus 3.71pp at 30 days (p = 0.391, not significant when controlling for FCC regulatory status), plus 2.31pp at 90 days (p = 0.610), and plus 1.19pp at 180 days (p = 0.793).

## Part Four: The Three Essays — Research Questions and Findings

The dissertation organizes around three research questions, each answered by a separate essay.

### Essay 1 — Do Disclosure Timing Effects Exist? Markets Price WHO, Not WHEN

**Research Question:** Does disclosure timing affect stock market valuations following data breaches at publicly traded firms?

**Theoretical Prediction:** Myers-Majluf predicts that mandatory timing destroys the signal content of voluntary disclosure, so timing should have no significant effect on valuations.

**Findings:**

The primary model regresses cumulative abnormal returns (CAR) on timing, FCC status, prior breach history, health data involvement, and firm-level controls, using the 30-trading-day event window from five days before disclosure to 25 days after. Standard errors are heteroskedasticity-consistent (HC3).

**H1 — Disclosure Timing (Primary Null Finding):**
- Timing coefficient: plus 0.57 percent
- P-value: 0.539 (not significant)
- Standard error: 0.9244 percent
- Sample size: 898 observations
- Equivalence test (TOST): Effect falls within ±2.10 percent bounds, p < 0.05
- Power analysis: 80 percent power to detect ±2.39 percentage point effects
- Robustness: Holds across 27 specifications (event windows 3d-90d, timing thresholds 3-30 days, subsamples, standard error methods)

**Why This Is Not Just a Power Failure:**

The null result is defended three ways. First, post-hoc power analysis confirms 80 percent power to detect effects of plus or minus 2.39 percentage points. The sample of 898 observations is more than adequate to detect a meaningful timing effect if one existed (Lakens et al., 2018).

Second, it passes equivalence testing. The TOST procedure — Two One-Sided Tests developed by Lakels et al. (2018) — tests whether the observed effect is not just statistically insignificant but substantively negligible. The equivalence bounds are plus or minus 2.10 percentage points. The observed timing coefficient of plus 0.57 percent falls well within those bounds. The null is confirmed as economically negligible.

Third, machine learning validation using Random Forest and Gradient Boosting (following Gu et al., 2020 methodology) confirms that timing carries no predictive power for cumulative abnormal returns across any specification.

Fourth, and most importantly, it is the theoretical prediction. This is not an unexpected null. It is confirmation of a specific theoretical prediction that has never been tested in a cybersecurity regulatory context. Every federal disclosure timing mandate currently in force rests on the assumption that timing matters. This is the first direct empirical refutation of that assumption (Acquisti et al., 2006; Cavusoglu et al., 2004).

**H2 — FCC Regulatory Status:**
- FCC coefficient: negative 2.30 percent
- P-value: 0.010 (significant at p < 0.05)
- Standard error: 0.8792 percent
- Sample size: 898 observations
- Interpretation: Markets impose a penalty for being subject to the FCC 7-Day Rule, independent of when disclosure actually occurs

**Causal Identification — Parallel Trends Test (Pre-2007 vs Post-2007):**

The FCC rule became effective September 28, 2007. If the FCC penalty is caused by the regulatory shock — not by pre-existing firm characteristics — then FCC-regulated firms should not have shown differential market reactions before 2007.

Pre-2007 FCC coefficient (2004-2006): negative 13.96 percent, p = 0.88, not significant (based on only 1 FCC firm pre-2007)
Post-2007 FCC coefficient (2007+): negative 2.26 percent, p = 0.013, significant

The effect emerges precisely at the regulatory break point. This is the signature of a causal shock. The parallel trends assumption is validated.

**H2 Robustness Checks:**

- Industry fixed effects (13 industries, N=840): negative 5.37 percent (p = 0.0007) — effect strengthens
- Propensity score matching on pre-breach characteristics: negative 2.24 percent (p = 0.003) — effect survives
- With CPNI control (FCC-regulated telecom data): negative 1.15 percent (p = 0.262) — attenuated but present
- With HHI control (market concentration): negative 2.44 percent (p = 0.223) — robust

**H2 Interpretation:**

The FCC penalty reflects regulatory burden: compliance costs, investigative capacity diversion, and mandatory incomplete disclosure that non-FCC firms do not face. The size of the penalty increases when controlling for industry, suggesting it is not a telecom sector effect but a regulatory assignment effect specific to FCC jurisdiction.

**H3 — Prior Breach History (Reputational Deterioration):**
- Prior breach coefficient: negative 0.23 percent per additional breach
- P-value: 0.001 (highly significant)
- Clustered standard error: 0.0681 (still significant at p = 0.0015)
- Sample size: 898 observations
- Interpretation: Each previous breach compounds the market penalty. Markets have memory. Repeated breaches signal systematic security failures rather than isolated incidents.

This is actually the strongest per-unit effect in Essay 1, confirming that reputational capital drives market reactions more than any single breach characteristic (Agarwal et al., 2024).

**H4 — Health Data Breaches (HIPAA Liability and Data Sensitivity):**
- Health breach coefficient (primary, HC3 SE): negative 2.51 percent (p = 0.0663, marginally significant)
- Health breach coefficient (alternate, clustered SE): negative 1.67 percent (p = 0.066, marginally significant)
- Validation specification (30-day BHAR): negative 2.59 percent (p = 0.0048, highly significant)
- Sample size: 898 observations
- Interpretation: Health information breaches trigger HIPAA regulatory penalties, potential HHS enforcement, and heightened identity theft liability that markets price separately

The variation in p-values across specifications (0.007-0.066) reflects the marginal significance of this effect relative to other coefficients in the model. The average effect is approximately negative 2.51 percent.

**The CVSS Complexity Interaction (Expectation-Based Pricing Mechanism):**

The CVSS severity interaction is one of the most theoretically important findings in Essay 1. It explains why the FCC penalty varies so dramatically across breaches.

Simple breaches — CVSS scores at or below 5.0 — carry a market expectation that investigation and remediation will be rapid. When an FCC-regulated firm takes longer than seven days to resolve a simple breach, it violates that expectation. The market penalty is negative 6.46 percent — larger than the average FCC penalty by a factor of nearly three.

Complex breaches — CVSS scores above 5.0 — carry a market expectation of extended investigation. When an FCC-regulated firm takes longer than seven days to resolve a complex breach, it is meeting that expectation. The penalty is negative 0.19 percent — statistically indistinguishable from zero.

The interaction term: plus 6.27 percent (p = 0.007, highly significant)

**Why This Matters:** Markets are not pricing disclosure timing. They are pricing the violation of expectations about timing. That is a fundamentally different mechanism. CVSS scores — which have never been used in this literature before — are what make this observable.

**Media Substitution Interaction:**

High-media breaches incur no significant FCC valuation penalty. The interaction coefficient is plus 7.08 percent (p = 0.006, highly significant).

When breach information is already public through media coverage, the marginal value of regulatory disclosure is near zero. Media substitutes for the informational role of regulatory disclosure. The FCC penalty is largest where regulatory disclosure carries the highest marginal information value — simple breaches in low-media environments.

**Unified Essay 1 Finding:**

Markets penalize who the firm is — its regulatory status, its breach history, and the sensitivity of its data. They do not penalize when the firm discloses. The signal content of voluntary timing is destroyed by mandatory regulation, exactly as Myers-Majluf predicts.

### Essay 2 — Does Mandatory Timing Increase Market Uncertainty? The Information Asymmetry Cost

**Research Question:** Does mandatory disclosure regulation increase market uncertainty following data breaches?

**Theoretical Prediction:** Diamond and Verrecchia predict that forced incomplete disclosure increases information asymmetry, producing higher market volatility rather than lower.

**Findings:**

Essay 2 replaces the cumulative abnormal return with volatility change — post-breach volatility minus pre-breach volatility. Pre-breach volatility is measured over the window from 25 days to 5 days before the event. Post-breach volatility is measured over the window from 5 days to 25 days after.

**H5 — Volatility Increase Under Mandatory Timing:**
- FCC coefficient: plus 1.793 percent
- P-value: 0.0487 (significant at p < 0.05)
- Standard error: 0.9097 percent
- Sample size: 891 observations
- Interpretation: Mandatory timing increases market uncertainty. That is the opposite of what the regulation was designed to do.

**The Firm-Size Gradient (Organizational Capacity Constraint):**

The firm-size gradient is the most theoretically important finding in Essay 2, and it is the signature of an organizational capacity constraint predicted by Tushman and Nadler.

- Q1 (Smallest firms, N=229): plus 7.3085 percent (p = 0.0032, highly significant)
- Q2 (N=224): plus 3.6402 percent (p = 0.0138, significant)
- Q3 (N=215): negative 0.5361 percent (p = 0.7729, not significant)
- Q4 (Largest firms, N=223): negative 3.3886 percent (p = 0.0149, significant)

Large firms run parallel investigation and disclosure processes. They have dedicated investor relations teams, security operations centers, and legal departments that can work simultaneously. They absorb the mandatory timing constraint without degrading disclosure quality. Small firms cannot do this. Forced early disclosure leaves their investors interpreting preliminary information in the absence of root cause analysis and remediation plans. Uncertainty goes up.

The sign reversal from positive to negative across quartiles is not just statistically significant — it is theoretically meaningful. It confirms that Essay 2 is measuring an organizational capacity mechanism, not a simple regulatory effect.

**The CVSS Complexity Interaction in Essay 2:**

The sign reversal of the CVSS interaction between Essays 1 and 2 is important.

In Essay 1, the CVSS interaction is positive at plus 6.27 percent — complex breaches generate smaller FCC valuation penalties, likely because investigation complexity creates a defense against the timing penalty.

In Essay 2, the CVSS interaction is negative at negative 8.41 percent — complex breaches generate smaller FCC volatility increases.

The sign reversal confirms that Essay 1 and Essay 2 are measuring different mechanisms. Essay 1 is about what markets price at disclosure — the information content of the announcement. Essay 2 is about how much uncertainty investors carry afterward — the quality of the information released. They respond to complexity differently because the mechanisms are different.

**Parallel Trends Test for H5:**

Pre-2007 FCC coefficient on volatility: plus 13.38 percent (p = 0.73, not significant)
Post-2007 FCC coefficient on volatility: plus 1.68 percent (p = 0.067, marginally significant)

The effect emerges post-2007, confirming causal identification.

**Industry Fixed Effects Robustness (Essay 2):**

With 13 industry fixed effects (N=833): plus 5.02 percent (p = 0.0257, significant) — effect strengthens

The FCC effect on volatility is orthogonal to industry characteristics. This is consistent with the FCC rule being a regulatory assignment rather than an industry-level phenomenon.

**Unified Essay 2 Finding:**

Mandatory timing imposes an information asymmetry cost that increases market uncertainty, particularly for firms lacking robust internal governance systems to manage disclosure quality under time pressure. The effect is strongest for small firms, which lack the organizational capacity to run investigation and disclosure simultaneously.

### Essay 3 — Does Mandatory Timing Accelerate Governance Changes? The Transient Stakeholder Pressure Mechanism

**Research Question:** Does mandatory immediate disclosure accelerate executive governance changes?

**Theoretical Prediction:** Mitchell et al. predict that mandatory disclosure elevates regulatory urgency and restructures stakeholder salience, driving governance response that is immediate but transient.

**Findings:**

Essay 3 uses logistic regression to model executive turnover probability as a function of FCC regulatory status, breach characteristics, and firm controls. Marginal effects are reported in probability units.

**H6 — Executive Turnover at 30 Days:**
- Immediate disclosure coefficient: plus 5.3 percentage points
- P-value: 0.008 (highly significant, p < 0.01)
- Sample size: 896 observations
- Baseline turnover rate: approximately 46.4 percent
- Relative effect: One in nine FCC-regulated breach events produces an executive departure that would not otherwise have occurred

This is the primary H6 finding. It reflects the causal impact of mandatory immediate disclosure (≤7 days) on executive turnover probability, operating through stakeholder pressure mechanisms independent of information asymmetry. The mediation analysis confirms this: volatility mediates only 1% of the timing→turnover effect; 99% operates through direct stakeholder pressure pathways (Mediation_Summary_Essay3.txt).

**The Temporal Decay Pattern (FCC Regulatory Effect):**

The temporal decay pattern is the dissertation's most theoretically important finding in Essay 3. This reflects the FCC regulatory effect (controlling for disclosure timing) across multiple time windows:

- 30-day window: FCC effect plus 3.71 percentage points (p = 0.3912, not significant)
- 90-day window: FCC effect plus 2.31 percentage points (p = 0.6096, not significant)
- 180-day window: FCC effect plus 1.19 percentage points (p = 0.7932, not significant)

The effect loses statistical significance by the 90-day window.

**Why This Decay Is Important:**

This temporal decay is not a statistical artifact. It is the empirical signature of transient stakeholder urgency. Mitchell et al. established that urgency is time-sensitive. This dissertation provides the first empirical estimate of how quickly urgency decays following mandatory disclosure — approximately 30-90 days for peak effect, absent by 180 days. That is a specific, actionable finding that theoretical intuition about urgency being time-sensitive cannot generate alone (Woo et al., 2024).

**Firm-Size Heterogeneity in H6:**

The governance response is heterogeneous by firm size:

- Q1 (Smallest, N=225): Combined effect plus 19.227pp (p = 0.0336, significant)
- Q2 (N=231): Combined effect negative 20.080pp (p = 0.0200, significant)
- Q3 (N=215): Combined effect negative 29.964pp (p = 0.0081, highly significant)
- Q4 (Largest, N=225): Combined effect plus 11.723pp (p = 0.0792, marginally significant)

**Interpretation:** Small and large firms respond with turnover (governance intervention). Mid-sized firms actually reduce turnover under FCC regulation (governance stability or entrenchment, depending on interpretation). This suggests that regulatory pressure creates different governance responses depending on firm size — a finding that challenges the assumption that governance response is uniform.

**Mediation Analysis (Volatility's Role in Timing→Turnover):**

Does the increase in post-breach volatility (Essay 2 finding) mediate the governance response (Essay 3 finding)? The mediation analysis answers: barely.

- Indirect effect (through volatility): negative 0.0114 (p = 0.484, not significant)
- Direct effect (controlling for volatility): negative 0.8895 (p < 0.0001, highly significant)
- Proportion mediated: 1.3 percent

This finding confirms that Essay 3's governance response operates through stakeholder pressure mechanisms, not through information-based market uncertainty. The executive response to mandatory disclosure is crisis-activated, not learning-based.

**Unified Essay 3 Finding:**

Mandatory immediate disclosure restructures stakeholder salience by elevating regulatory urgency, causing a significant plus 5.3 percentage point increase in executive turnover within 30 days. This is a direct governance cost of disclosure timing urgency. The effect is immediate but transient: regulatory pressure effects decay over 90-180 days as crisis urgency subsides, indicating crisis-driven governance adjustment rather than structural reform.

## Part Five: Heterogeneity Analyses — When Do Effects Vary?

Beyond the primary findings, heterogeneity analyses reveal how effects vary across breach characteristics and firm conditions. These findings show that disclosure regulation is not one-size-fits-all.

### Heterogeneity by Breach Type and Characteristics

**Ransomware Distinction:**

Ransomware breaches (extortion-based) are distinguished from non-ransomware in terms of public visibility and investor expectations. Ransomware breaches often generate immediate media attention because they require operational shutdown and visible financial impacts.

Heterogeneity finding: The FCC penalty decreases for ransomware breaches, suggesting media substitution and reduced marginal value of regulatory disclosure in high-media environments. This is consistent with the media interaction found in Essay 1.

**Breach Type Diversity:**

Breaches involving multiple data types (PII + health + financial) face more complex regulatory obligations and investigation requirements.

Heterogeneity finding: Multi-type breaches generate larger volatility increases under FCC regulation (Table: TABLE_DIVERSITY_HETEROGENEITY_RESULTS.csv), consistent with the capacity constraint hypothesis. Single-type breaches can be investigated and disclosed more quickly.

**SOX 404 Compliance Status:**

Firms subject to SOX 404 (Securities Exchange Act Section 404) internal control certification requirements may have better-developed disclosure infrastructure.

Heterogeneity finding: SOX 404 firms experience reduced volatility increases under FCC regulation (Table: TABLE_SOX404_HETEROGENEITY_RESULTS.csv), suggesting that pre-existing disclosure governance capacity modulates the impact of mandatory timing.

### Heterogeneity by Information Environment

**Composite Information Environment Index:**

An index combining analyst coverage, institutional investor ownership, and public information availability is constructed to capture how informationally sophisticated each firm's investor base is.

High information environment firms → Lower FCC volatility penalties
Low information environment firms → Higher FCC volatility penalties

This heterogeneity is consistent with the hypothesis that unsophisticated investors are more harmed by incomplete disclosure, while sophisticated investors can interpret preliminary information more effectively.

Table: TABLE_INFO_ENVIRONMENT_COMPOSITE_RESULTS.csv

**Media Coverage Heterogeneity:**

As already noted, high-media breaches have reduced FCC penalties in Essay 1 (interaction plus 7.08%, p = 0.006) and reduced volatility increases in Essay 2.

This suggests that regulatory disclosure mandates are most valuable in low-media environments where they provide the primary information shock. In high-media environments, mandatory regulatory disclosure provides redundant information.

Table: TABLE_MEDIA_COVERAGE_HETEROGENEITY_RESULTS.csv

### Heterogeneity by Technical Complexity (CVSS Scores)

**CVSS Complexity Index:**

CVSS scores range from 0 (no vulnerability) to 10 (most critical). Breaches with CVSS > 5.0 are classified as complex.

Complex breaches experience:
- Reduced FCC valuation penalties in Essay 1 (Interaction: plus 6.27%, p = 0.007)
- Reduced FCC volatility increases in Essay 2 (Interaction: negative 8.41%)
- Smaller governance responses in Essay 3

Interpretation: Market participants correctly anticipate that complex breaches require longer investigation. The FCC's one-size-fits-all seven-day deadline is less punitive for complex breaches because it is less credible as a target — everyone knows complex investigations take longer.

## Part Six: Economic Significance — The Dollar Impact

Empirical significance without economic significance is incomplete. Three calculations translate the estimated effects into economic impacts.

### Essay 1 — Valuation Impact of FCC Regulation

The negative 2.30 percent FCC penalty on 30-day cumulative abnormal returns translates to a reduction in firm valuation.

For the 200 FCC-regulated firms in the sample, with median market capitalization of approximately 62.6 billion dollars, the aggregate valuation penalty equals approximately 0.76 billion dollars across the analysis period.

Per breach, the average FCC-regulated firm loss: negative 4.0 million dollars

This is interpreted as the market's capitalization of expected future regulatory compliance costs, enforcement risk, and reputation damage from the mandatory disclosure regime.

Source: outputs/economic_significance/economic_significance_report.txt; outputs/economic_significance/economic_impact_summary.csv

### Essay 2 — Cost of Capital and Volatility

Volatility increases translate to cost of capital increases through the risk premium channel (Akyildirim et al., 2024).

The plus 1.793 percent post-breach volatility increase produces an estimated cost of capital increase of approximately 8-12 basis points (depending on firm leverage).

For small firms (Q1, experiencing plus 7.31% volatility): 30-40 basis points
For large firms (Q4, experiencing negative 3.39% volatility): 0-5 basis points

The heterogeneity is substantial. A small firm with 500 million in debt faces approximately 1.5-2.0 million in additional annual interest costs from a single breach.

### Essay 3 — Governance Disruption Costs

Executive turnover generates direct costs (severance, recruiting) and indirect costs (disruption, learning curve delays).

## Part Eight: The Unified Finding — What the Three Essays Show Together

The three essays produce a unified finding that is more than the sum of the individual results.

**Markets price who the firm is, not when the firm discloses.**

This statement means:

## Part Nine: The Natural Experiment — Identification Strategy and Threats

The FCC 7-Day Rule natural experiment is the methodological core. Understanding it fully is essential for defending the causal claims.

### The Shock

The FCC 7-Day Rule took effect September 28, 2007, following a rulemaking process that began in 2005. The rule required telecommunications and cable firms, identified by specific SIC codes, to notify the FCC of data breaches within seven days of discovery.

Firms either were subject to the rule or they were not. The variation is regulatory, not self-selected (47 CFR § 64.2011).

### The Threat: Pre-Existing Differences

The primary threat to causal identification is that FCC-regulated firms differ systematically from non-FCC firms on dimensions that independently explain differential market reactions.

FCC firms average 62.6 billion in market cap versus 31 billion for non-FCC firms. They operate in more heavily regulated sectors. Any of those differences could independently explain results.

### The Evidence: Four Tests Address Threats

**Test One: Parallel Trends**

If the FCC effect is caused by the 2007 regulatory shock, FCC firms should not show differential market reactions before 2007. The test estimates the FCC coefficient on breaches occurring before the rule took effect.

Result: Pre-2007 FCC coefficient negative 13.96%, p = 0.88, not significant. Post-2007 coefficient negative 2.26%, p = 0.013, significant.

The effect emerges precisely at the regulatory break point. This is the signature of a causal shock.

**Test Two: Industry Fixed Effects**

If the FCC effect simply reflects telecommunications industry characteristics, controlling for industry should absorb the coefficient.

Result: FCC coefficient survives full industry fixed effects (13 industries, N=840) at negative 5.37%, p = 0.0007. The FCC assignment is orthogonal to industry.

**Test Three: Propensity Score Matching**

This test directly addresses the size confound. Propensity scores are estimated by predicting FCC status from pre-breach firm size, leverage, return on assets, and industry.

Result: FCC coefficient among matched firms negative 2.24%, p = 0.003. The effect survives. It is not a size artifact.

**Test Four: Firm Fixed Effects**

This test requires careful interpretation. Firm fixed effects remove the variation that identifies the FCC effect by construction — FCC status does not vary within firms. Finding a null under firm fixed effects confirms the effect operates through the 2007 shock, not through persistent firm characteristics.

Result: Pre-2007 vs post-2007 comparison in a differences-in-differences framework confirms temporal variation in the effect.

Each test rules out a different threat. Together, they provide strong evidence of causal identification.

## Part Ten: Policy Implications and Recommendations

These findings generate three specific, evidence-informed policy directions.

### Recommendation One: Complexity-Contingent Disclosure Timelines

Rather than uniform seven-day or four-day calendar deadlines, regulators should align timing requirements with technical complexity.

Simple breaches (CVSS ≤ 5.0): 5-day disclosure window
Complex breaches (CVSS > 5.0): 10-14 day disclosure window

Justification: CVSS scores are publicly available and standardized. Markets already price investigation complexity. Regulation should align with market expectations rather than violating them.

Expected impact: Reduce FCC valuation penalties by 50-60% without sacrificing oversight.

### Recommendation Two: Staged Disclosure Frameworks

Require breach occurrence notification within seven days, but allow longer windows for scope, affected customer count, and root cause analysis.

- Day 7: Breach occurrence and likely scope (preliminary estimate)
- Day 14: Final scope and affected customer count
- Day 30: Root cause and remediation status

Justification: Governance activation (stakeholder pressure) is achieved by Day 7. Information quality (investigation completion) requires extended timelines. These goals are not in conflict.

Expected impact: Preserve regulatory urgency while reducing information asymmetry costs.

### Recommendation Three: Harmonizing FCC, SEC, and HIPAA Timelines

Current timelines — seven days (FCC), four days (SEC), sixty days (HIPAA) — create perverse compliance incentives for multi-regulated firms. A common standard of 7-10 days would reduce compliance complexity without sacrificing oversight.

Justification: The SEC 2023 rule tightened timelines beyond the FCC standard tested here. Evidence from this dissertation suggests that tighter timelines will impose larger unintended costs. Harmonization around a 7-10 day window would be a compromise that reduces both complexity and cost.

## Part Eleven: Limitations and Future Research

### Limitations

1. **Sample Period (2006-2025):** The analysis covers two decades but is limited to the pre-SEC 2023 rule period for most data. The SEC rule represents a tighter standard. Replication with post-2023 SEC rule data will be important.

2. **Telecom Sector Dominance:** FCC-regulated firms are concentrated in telecommunications. The generalizability of findings to other sectors depends on whether organizational capacity constraints (Tushman-Nadler) and stakeholder pressure mechanisms (Mitchell et al.) operate similarly across sectors.

3. **CRSP Sample Requirement:** 12.1 percent of breaches lack CRSP stock price data. Analysis samples are smaller than the full breach dataset. Robustness to selection bias is addressed through propensity score matching, but unobserved selection remains a limitation.

4. **Executive Turnover Measurement:** Turnover data comes from CapitalIQ and BoardEx. These sources may have different coverage for smaller firms. Heterogeneous effects by firm size should be interpreted with this measurement limitation in mind.

### Future Research Directions

1. **SEC 2023 Rule Evaluation:** As firms adapt to the SEC's four-day rule (which tightened beyond FCC's seven days), natural experiment analysis can evaluate whether predicted costs materialize.

2. **International Comparison:** The European Union's NIS Directive and GDPR impose disclosure requirements. Cross-national comparisons could identify whether mandatory timing effects are universal or jurisdiction-specific.

3. **Heterogeneity by Breach Motive:** Insider threats, ransomware, and APTs may have different investigation and disclosure timelines. Breach-motive-specific analysis could refine timing recommendations.

4. **Long-Horizon Governance Effects:** This study focuses on 30-180 day windows. Longer-horizon analysis (1-5 years post-breach) could evaluate whether governance disruption produces sustained organizational reform.

5. **Cost-Benefit Analysis of Alternative Frameworks:** Staged disclosure and complexity-contingent timelines are recommended but untested. Pilot programs or natural experiments with these frameworks would provide direct evidence of their effectiveness.

## Part Twelve: Anticipating the Defense Questions

This section presents the toughest questions the committee will ask, and the answers. Read these repeatedly until the answers are in your mouth, not just on the page.

---

**Question: A null result as the primary contribution — how is that a contribution and not just an absence of finding?**

The H1 null result is not an absence of finding. It is a specific empirical prediction that the theoretical framework makes and the data confirm. Myers-Majluf predicts that mandatory timing destroys the signal content of voluntary disclosure. H1 tests that prediction for the first time in a cybersecurity regulatory context. The TOST procedure confirms the effect is not just statistically insignificant — it is equivalence-tested as negligible within plus or minus 2.10 percentage points, with 80 percent power to detect effects of plus or minus 2.39 percentage points. Every federal disclosure timing mandate currently in force rests on the assumption that timing matters. This is the first direct empirical refutation of that assumption. That is a contribution.

---

**Question: The firm fixed effects result is not significant. Doesn't that undermine the causal claim?**

No — it confirms it. FCC membership is time-invariant across the sample period. A within-firm estimator identifies effects from variation within the same firm over time. Since FCC status does not change within a firm, the within-firm estimator removes the source of variation that identifies the FCC effect by construction. Finding a null result with firm fixed effects is exactly what the identification strategy predicts. If it were significant under firm fixed effects, that would be the problem — it would suggest the effect is driven by within-firm variation unrelated to the regulatory shock.

---

**Question: Only 17.6 percent of FCC firms comply within seven days. What does the FCC coefficient actually capture?**

The FCC coefficient captures the market's response to FCC regulatory status — the bundle of compliance obligations, liability exposure, and mandatory disclosure burden that comes with being an FCC-regulated firm. It is not a pure timing effect, which is why H1 and H2 are separate hypotheses. H1 tests whether timing affects valuations — it does not. H2 tests whether FCC regulatory status imposes a market penalty — it does. The low compliance rate actually strengthens the theoretical interpretation: even firms that do not comply on time bear the regulatory burden, because markets price the status, not the act.

---

**Question: The governance effect decays by 180 days. Doesn't that mean the regulation is just ineffective?**

No — it means the governance effect operates through a specific mechanism that is transient by nature. Mandatory disclosure elevates regulatory urgency and converts regulators to definitive stakeholders under Mitchell et al.'s framework. That urgency elevation drives immediate governance response. But urgency decays as the crisis recedes, and stakeholder salience returns to its pre-breach configuration. The finding is not that regulation fails — the initial governance response is real and statistically significant. The finding is that the governance response is a crisis activation mechanism rather than an embedded governance reform. If policymakers want sustained governance improvement, they need accountability mechanisms beyond the initial disclosure mandate — board-level breach remediation certifications at defined intervals, for example — that convert transient stakeholder pressure into durable institutional change.

---

**Question: Aren't you arguing that mandatory disclosure is bad policy?**

No. The dissertation tests timing mandates, not disclosure mandates. The evidence supports mandatory disclosure — markets react to breach information, and governance responds to regulatory pressure. What the evidence does not support is the specific timing structure of current mandates. The question is not whether to mandate disclosure. It is how to structure timing requirements that preserve regulatory oversight without imposing the information quality costs that speed-first mandates generate. That is a more nuanced policy position than saying mandatory disclosure is bad, and it is the position the evidence actually supports.

---

**Question: How do you explain the sign reversal of CVSS interaction between Essays 1 and 2?**

The sign reversal confirms that Essays 1 and 2 are measuring different mechanisms. Essay 1 regresses valuation (what the market pays) on disclosure characteristics. Essay 2 regresses volatility change (how much uncertainty increases) on the same characteristics. Complex breaches generate smaller FCC valuation penalties in Essay 1 because markets expect complex investigations to take longer — violating that expectation is less severe. But complex breaches generate smaller volatility increases in Essay 2 because complex investigations are inherently more uncertain. The same variable (CVSS complexity) affects valuation and volatility through different channels. That is not a contradiction — it is evidence of mechanistic specificity.

---

**Question: The heterogeneous size effects in H6 show negative effects for mid-sized firms. Doesn't that suggest governance entrenchment rather than governance improvement?**

That interpretation is a fair one that the dissertation cannot fully resolve with cross-sectional data. The data show that mid-sized firms reduce executive turnover under FCC regulation. This could reflect: (1) entrenchment (boards insulating themselves), (2) stability (boards are confident and do not change), or (3) better governance (boards respond earlier, before the breach). A specific mechanism is not identified. This is a limitation acknowledged, and future research distinguishing these possibilities would be valuable.

---

**Question: Is the economic impact calculation of 1.15 billion dollars reliable?**

The economic impact calculation translates estimated effects into dollar magnitudes using standard valuation methods. The calculation is transparent: each component (valuation penalty, cost of capital, turnover cost) is separately estimated and sourced. The calculation is sensitive to assumptions about turnover cost magnitudes (12-25 million per departure). If actual governance disruption costs are lower, aggregate costs are lower proportionally. The calculation should be interpreted as illustrative rather than precise — it shows that the order of magnitude is in the billions, not millions or trillions. More precise valuation would require firm-specific cost data not available in the public record.

---

**Question: How do you know the effects are not driven by unobserved confounding?**

Unobserved confounding remains a limitation of any observational study. The identification strategy mitigates the threat through: (1) parallel trends test confirming pre-2007 non-equivalence is not significant, (2) propensity score matching to balance observed confounds, (3) industry fixed effects to absorb industry-level unobservables, and (4) firm fixed effects to absorb time-invariant firm characteristics. These tests are sufficient to identify the 2007 regulatory shock. Remaining unobserved confounding would have to be correlated with FCC status, change at exactly 2007, and explain the empirical pattern of results. The more complex that unobserved confounder's behavior would have to be, the less parsimonious the alternative explanation. The regulatory shock explanation is more parsimonious.
