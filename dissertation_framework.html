"Does immediate disclosure of data breaches

Why This Question Works for a Dissertation
1. Practical Importance: Every breached company faces this exact decision. Regulators are actively debating disclosure timeline requirements with real financial and reputational stakes.
2. Theoretical Tension: Immediate disclosure signals transparency and limits information asymmetry (Myers-Majluf 1984). Delayed disclosure allows investigation and remediation planning. Theory predicts competing forces.
3. Non-Obvious Answer: Results show it's context-dependent. FCC-regulated firms experience -3.60%*** larger penalties despite faster compliance, suggesting mandatory immediate disclosure can backfire when markets expect perfect information.
Theoretical Foundation: Information Asymmetry Theory (Myers & Majluf 1984)
Managers possess private information about breach severity that markets lack.
Disclosure timing decisions signal information quality and resolve uncertainty.
Extended with regulatory signaling theory and governance mechanisms.

How the Three Essays Answer the Central Question
Essay 1: Theory & Comprehensive Analysis
Develops the theoretical framework predicting when immediate vs. delayed disclosure is optimal based on information asymmetry, stakeholder pressure, and regulatory environment. Tests all moderators to identify boundary conditions.

Key Finding: Immediate disclosure has minimal direct benefit (+1.47%, p=0.180 n.s.), BUT regulatory context matters enormously. FCC-regulated firms experience -3.60%*** larger penalties (p=0.003), suggesting forced immediacy creates expectations penalty that outweighs transparency benefits.
Essay 2: Evidence on Market Reactions
Deep dive into event study methodology and market reaction heterogeneity. Examines different event windows (5-day, 30-day), robustness checks, and timing of information incorporation into stock prices.

Key Findings: Market penalties vary dramatically by context (-43% to +34% range). Health data breaches create -4.32%*** additional penalty. Prior breaches add -0.11%*** per incident (cumulative reputation damage). Executive turnover adds -2.29%** signal. Firm profitability (ROA) provides +25.83%*** cushion.
Essay 3: Mechanism Analysis (Information Asymmetry)
Reveals the mechanism linking timing to reactions through information asymmetry. Uses volatility change as proxy for uncertainty resolution. Tests governance moderation hypotheses.

Key Finding: FCC regulation increases post-breach volatility by +4.96%*** (p=0.001), confirming that regulatory mandates increase information asymmetry rather than resolve it. Immediate disclosure under regulation creates dual penalty: (1) expectations violation, (2) incomplete information disclosure.
THE DISSERTATION'S ANSWER
"It depends on regulatory context and breach characteristics. Immediate disclosure does NOT universally benefit firms (H1 not supported). Instead, regulatory mandates (FCC -3.60%***), breach type (health data -4.32%***), and firm reputation (prior breaches -0.11%*** each) determine market penalties. The optimal strategy balances transparency benefits against expectations penalties under regulatory scrutiny."
COUNTERINTUITIVE FINDING #1:
Mandatory disclosure (FCC) creates LARGER market penalties (-3.60%***, p=0.003)
despite faster compliance, because regulation signals heightened duty violation expectations.
Markets penalize FCC firms more heavily for breach occurrence, not disclosure delay.
COUNTERINTUITIVE FINDING #2:
Prior breaches create cumulative reputation damage (-0.11%*** per breach, p=0.002),
NOT learning effects. Repeat offenders face compounding penalties, contrary to H3 prediction.
Revised Interpretation: Markets view repeat breaches as evidence of persistent governance failure.

RESEARCH DESIGN & SAMPLE
Total Sample: 1,054 data breaches (2006-2025, 19 years)
Essay 2 (Event Study): 926 breaches with CRSP data (87.9% coverage)
Essay 3 (Volatility): 916 breaches with volatility data (86.9% coverage)

Treatment Group: 190 FCC-regulated firms (19.0%)
Control Group: 864 non-FCC firms (81.0%)

Sample Attrition Analysis: Excluded breaches differ significantly on firm size (p<0.001),
ROA (p=0.015), FCC status (p=0.010), and prior breaches (p<0.001).
Results generalize to publicly-traded U.S. firms with available market data.

Methodology: Event study (30-day CAR primary, 5-day robustness) +
OLS regression with HC3 heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors +
Volatility change analysis (information asymmetry proxy)
DISSERTATION CONTRIBUTIONS
1. Theoretical: Extends Myers-Majluf information asymmetry theory to cybersecurity disclosure context, identifying dual mechanism of regulatory signaling (credibility premium vs. expectations penalty).

2. Empirical: First large-scale test (1,054 breaches) of FCC regulation impact on breach disclosure market reactions. Discovers counterintuitive expectations penalty effect (-3.60%***).

3. Methodological: Novel enrichment variables (prior breach history, executive turnover tracking, health data classification, regulatory enforcement) advance cybersecurity disclosure literature.

4. Practical: Informs regulatory policy debate on mandatory disclosure timelines. Shows regulation can backfire by creating expectations penalty rather than transparency benefits.
